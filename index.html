Method and Theory Today

Arguably, method and theory form the foundation of any academic field or discipline. These ideas complex, however, when one sees that there is no single method and theory for any discipline. In fact, it can be argued that the diverse methods and theories are what keeps academia vibrant. Nonetheless, method and theory provides a framework for disciplines, and through an exploration of Digital Archaeology it is clear that there are some concerns arise that make it distinct from traditional archaeology. Moreover, it is useful to explore these issues and consider how they may develop in the future.

“Digital Archaeology is Here” by Andre Costopoulos1 explores how to do archaeology digitally and what it means to be a digital archaeologist today. From understanding the digitization of archaeology to the use of digital tools, Costopoulos effectively demonstrates how archaeologists have been on the cusp of new media and communications technology for many years, and this has only increased over time. Moreover, Costopoulos demonstrates how technology is increasingly becoming a key factor in the method and theory of digital archaeologists. Additionally, the problems facing digital archaeologists are not new, he argues, but are indeed changes resulting from new technological developments. Overall, this article effectively demonstrates how the method and theory of digital archaeologists, while always faced with a host of issues, is clearly being affected by the digital age.

Eric C. Kansa’s “Click Here to Save Archaeology”2 explores a fundamental component of Digital Archaeology: data. In particular, he explores “slow data.” He claims slow data involves the digitized aspects of "slow archaeology," or “the notion that [archaeologists] should emphasize excellence in the research process.” Processing and interpreting data is becoming an increasingly important part of Digital Archaeology, and between “slow data” and “big data” archaeologists are increasingly dealing with large amounts of complex data. Thus, the methodology is changing. By adapting the idea of slow archaeology to data analysis, Kansa notes how archaeologists can “articulate more human and insightful approaches to the ‘datafication’ of archaeology.” New technologies, platforms, and so forth are not enough, according to Kansa, to “promote ethical practice or more nuanced understandings of the past.” Instead, Kansa claims there must be long-term institutional and financial mechanisms to further expand the field. In order to avoid the subversion of ethics and goals, efforts must be made to “work toward a better institutional foundation” that can effectively accommodate both traditional and digital archaeology. Clearly, archaeologists' methods must change as their concepts of data change.

In “A Digital Public Archaeology,” Lorna Richardson3 discusses the “limited amount of theoretical scrutiny” in Digital Public Archaeology. The ways in which research can be collected, shared, and discussed as a result of new technologies has created new expectations and opportunities for public archaeology, especially in regards to how participation and interaction can occur. With new opportunities, however, Richardson comments on the new responsibilities that will arise, especially in relation to ethical concerns. She concludes by stating that there is much potential for the Internet to guide archaeology and affect the “impact and presentation of Public Archaeology.” Additionally, she addresses how new methods and theories are contributing to a new relationship between archaeologists and the public as a result of digital technologies. Thus, just as new methods are emerging as a result of digitization and technology, theories are transforming too.

Method and Theory in the Future

As the discipline begins to change, so too must the methods and theories that digital archaeologists employ. Costopolous, Kansa, and Richardson explore important dimensions of Digital Archaeology and how methods and theories are becoming distinct from those of traditional archaeology as a result of the digitization of research and dissemination. The way archaeologists are approaching digitization, the transformation of data, and the impact of digital technologies on public archaeology, it is clear that the aims and practices of archaeology are changing significantly. As Digital Archaeology continues to develop, however, how will methods and theories be affected? As with technology, no one can know for sure. The above discussion, however, seems to point to the fact that archaeologists must be able and willing to adapt to changes in the discipline. Whether one views method and theory as shaping the discipline or vice versa, it is clear that Digital Archaeology is changing rapidly and will likely continue to in the future. Indeed, the transformation and development of Digital Archaeology will also have significant consequences on another key component of the discipline: the ethics.
